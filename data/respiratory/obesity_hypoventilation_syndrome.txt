Obesity hypoventilation syndrome
Obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS) is a complex
respiratory
disorder characterized by chronic hypoventilation, obesity, and sleep-disordered breathing, particularly obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). This condition, also known as Pickwickian syndrome, can result in significant morbidity and mortality if left untreated. This textbook entry aims to provide a comprehensive overview of the pathophysiology, clinical presentation, diagnosis, and management of OHS to help doctors better understand and manage this condition.

Pathophysiology
OHS results from a combination of factors related to obesity and its effects on the
respiratory
system. These factors include:
Impaired respiratory mechanics:
Excessive adipose tissue, particularly in the chest and abdominal regions, can restrict lung expansion and limit diaphragmatic movement, resulting in decreased lung volumes and increased work of breathing.
Altered ventilatory control:
OHS patients may have impaired central respiratory drive, leading to inadequate ventilation and subsequent hypoventilation, particularly during sleep.
Sleep-disordered breathing:
OSA is highly prevalent in OHS patients, contributing to nocturnal hypoventilation, intermittent hypoxia, and hypercapnia.
Hormonal and metabolic factors:
Obesity is associated with hormonal and metabolic abnormalities that can further exacerbate hypoventilation, such as leptin resistance and insulin resistance.

Clinical features
Patients with OHS typically present with the following signs and symptoms:
Obesity:
Body mass index (BMI) ≥30 kg/m2, often with central adiposity.
Daytime hypoventilation:
Manifests as dyspnea, exercise intolerance, and fatigue.
Sleep-disordered breathing:
Symptoms may include loud snoring, witnessed apneas, nocturnal choking or gasping, and excessive daytime sleepiness.
Morning headaches and cognitive dysfunction:
These symptoms may result from chronic hypercapnia and hypoxemia.
Signs of right heart failure:
Including peripheral edema, jugular venous distention, and hepatomegaly, due to chronic hypoxemia and pulmonary hypertension.

Diagnosis
The diagnosis of OHS is based on the following criteria:
Obesity:
BMI ≥30 kg/m2.
Hypoventilation:
Daytime arterial blood gas analysis demonstrating hypercapnia and hypoxemia in the absence of other causes.
Sleep-disordered breathing:
Polysomnography demonstrating the presence of OSA or other sleep-related breathing disorders.
Additional diagnostic tests may include pulmonary function tests, chest radiography, electrocardiography, and echocardiography to evaluate lung function, rule out alternative diagnoses, and assess the severity of the condition.

Differential diagnosis
Obesity hypoventilation syndrome (OHS) and obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) are both sleep-related breathing disorders that are often associated with obesity. However, they have distinct characteristics and clinical implications:
Obesity Hypoventilation Syndrome (OHS):
OHS is characterized by obesity, chronic hypoventilation (PaCO2 ≥ 45 mmHg), and sleep-disordered breathing, particularly OSA.
The pathophysiology of OHS involves a combination of impaired
respiratory
mechanics, altered ventilatory control, sleep-disordered breathing, and hormonal and metabolic factors.
Patients with OHS often experience daytime hypoventilation, which leads to chronic hypercapnia and hypoxemia, resulting in symptoms such as dyspnea, exercise intolerance, morning headaches, and cognitive dysfunction.
OHS can lead to serious complications, such as pulmonary hypertension, right heart failure, and increased risk of cardiovascular events.
Management of OHS typically includes weight loss, positive airway pressure therapy (CPAP or BiPAP), oxygen therapy, and pharmacological treatment to address underlying causes and complications.
Obstructive Sleep Apnea (OSA):
OSA is characterized by repetitive episodes of partial or complete upper airway obstruction during sleep, leading to disrupted breathing, sleep fragmentation, and intermittent hypoxemia.
The pathophysiology of OSA involves mechanical factors, such as obesity, fat deposition in the upper airway, and reduced airway muscle tone, which contribute to airway collapse during sleep.
Patients with OSA often experience loud snoring, witnessed apneas, nocturnal choking or gasping, and excessive daytime sleepiness.
OSA can lead to various complications, including hypertension, cardiovascular disease, stroke, impaired cognitive function, and increased risk of motor vehicle accidents.
Management of OSA primarily focuses on positive airway pressure therapy (CPAP), lifestyle modifications (weight loss, sleep position, and avoiding alcohol), and in some cases, oral appliances or surgical interventions.
In summary, OHS is a more complex disorder involving chronic hypoventilation, obesity, and sleep-disordered breathing, whereas OSA is specifically characterized by upper airway obstruction during sleep. OHS patients typically have OSA as part of their clinical presentation, but not all OSA patients have OHS. The management strategies for these conditions may overlap, but the approach for OHS is more comprehensive, addressing the multiple factors contributing to hypoventilation and its complications.

Management
The management of OHS involves addressing the underlying causes and complications, including:
Weight loss:
Encouraging lifestyle modifications, such as a balanced diet and regular physical activity, is crucial. Bariatric surgery may be considered in refractory cases or for patients with severe obesity.
Positive airway pressure therapy:
Continuous positive airway pressure (CPAP) or bilevel positive airway pressure (BiPAP) can be used to treat OSA and nocturnal hypoventilation.
Oxygen therapy:
Supplemental oxygen may be required for patients with persistent hypoxemia despite positive airway pressure therapy.
